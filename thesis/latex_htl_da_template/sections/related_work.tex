\section{Commercial Dart Scoring Systems}

\subsection{High-End Multi-Camera Systems}

\subsubsection{Technical Capabilities}

The technical capabilities of the commercial automatic scoring systems used in televised darts tournaments are based on the multi-camera triangulation approach. A minimum of three high-speed cameras are used to track the dart trajectory. Similar methodologies are widely used in sports tracking research where the reconstruction of fast-moving objects in three-dimensional space is required \cite{Osadchy2006}. The cameras are set to run at high frames per second to minimize the effect of motion blur while precisely determining the trajectory of the dart. 

To ensure robustness in the system with regard to the problem of occlusion, the dart trajectory is tracked through model-based tracking with Kalman filter estimation during the period of invisibility in flight \cite{Welch}. Finally, the region where the dart strikes the dartboard is identified through subpixel accuracy.

\subsubsection{Cost and Setup Requirements}

The technical capabilities of the system require the setup of multiple camera systems with precise camera calibration. The precise camera calibration is required to ensure the accuracy of the system. The accuracy of the system is compromised with the misalignment of the camera baseline \cite{Hartley2004}. 

Thus, the system setup is quite complex. The system is quite expensive. Thus, the system is not suitable for personal use. The complexity of the system setup is the reason for the need for alternative systems that are based on the use of a single camera system.

\subsection{Laser and Sensor-Based Systems}

\subsubsection{Hardware Dependencies}

The alternative commercial systems are based on the use of laser systems or vibration sensors attached to the dartboard. The dart location is identified through the interruption of the laser beams or the propagation of the vibration through the dartboard surface. Similar technical capabilities are used in the impact localization system where multiple sensors are used to track the impact location through the time difference in the impact detection system \cite{Schwingshackl2010}.

\subsubsection{Accuracy Characteristics}

Although sensor-based systems demonstrate reliability in terms of steel darts, they are subject to material dependency and mechanical damping effects. Wave propagation is affected by board wear and mounting conditions, and this results in systematic localization errors \cite{Schwingshackl2010}. Furthermore, this system is unable to identify bounce-outs or mid-air collisions and does not provide a visual verification.

\section{Research on Sports Object Tracking}

\subsection{Ball and Projectile Tracking}

\subsubsection{Motion Segmentation Techniques}

Motion segmentation is a widely applied technique in video analysis for segmenting out moving objects in a video stream. Background subtraction techniques, such as adaptive Gaussian mixture model-based methods, are commonly applied for background subtraction and segmenting out moving foreground objects from a static background \cite{Zivkovic2004}. 

These techniques are widely applied in sports object tracking for detecting moving balls or projectiles. Edge detection and object contour extraction are also applied for more accurate object localization by detecting edges in a video frame \cite{Canny1986}. For circular shapes, a Hough transform-based object detection technique is often applied \cite{Duda1972}. These techniques are efficient in detecting moving objects in a video stream and are a better alternative to deep learning-based methods.

\subsubsection{Trajectory Estimation Methods}

Once a moving object is detected in a video frame, its trajectory is estimated using state estimation methods. Kalman filtering is often applied for estimating a ball or projectile trajectory and correcting noise in the measurements \cite{Welch}. 

This is useful in detecting a temporarily occluded ball or estimating its impact position in a sports environment \cite{Osadchy2006}. The combination of object detection and trajectory estimation is useful in detecting a high-contrast, moving object in a video stream.

\section{Existing Open Source Projects}

\subsection{Dartboard Detection Tools}

\subsubsection{Marker-Based Alignment}

Several research prototypes make use of fiducial markers for camera calibration. ArUco markers allow for accurate pose estimation, especially in the case of partial occlusion. By using multiple markers attached to the board, it is possible to compute the homography between the image coordinates and the board coordinates. This way, it is possible to transform the impact point coordinates into standardized dart board coordinates regardless of the camera's point of view.

\subsubsection{Geometric Modeling Approaches}

After the camera has been calibrated, the majority of the systems make use of a geometric model of the dart board, which is composed of concentric rings and radial sectors. By determining the boundaries of the closest ring and sector, it is possible to determine the hit location. This approach replaces the machine learning-based method with a more deterministic approach, which relies on the mapping from pixel coordinates to board regions. This approach is more appropriate for real-time applications, especially considering the reduced computational requirements.

\section{Position of This Work in Current Literature}

\subsection{Comparison with Existing Approaches}

\subsubsection{Hardware Differences}

The existing approaches make use of either expensive multi-camera hardware or specialized sensor hardware. In contrast, the present work relies on the single camera hardware found on consumer devices, along with passive markers on the board. In comparison with the stereo vision-based approach presented in \cite{Hartley2004}, the present work avoids the need for multi-view camera calibration. In comparison with the vibration-based approach presented in \cite{Schwingshackl2010}, the present work avoids the need for board material or mounting conditions.

\subsubsection{Novelty of a Motion-Threshold Pipeline}

The present work makes use of motion segmentation, temporal thresholding, and geometric board modeling. In contrast to the existing machine learning-based approaches, the present work avoids the need for training data. Instead, the present work relies on classical computer vision techniques, such as background subtraction, as presented in \cite{Zivkovic2004}.
