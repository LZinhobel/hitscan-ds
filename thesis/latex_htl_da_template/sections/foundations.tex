\section{Camera Models and Projective Geometry}

\subsection{Perspective Projection}

\subsubsection{Pinhole Camera Model}

The camera image is a projection of the real-world scene onto the two-dimensional image plane. The projection is achieved according to the pinhole camera model, in which each point in the real world is projected to the image sensor through the camera center as a straight line \cite{Hartley2004}.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.65\linewidth]{pics/pinholeCamera.png}
    \caption{Pinhole camera projection model. Public domain image from Wikimedia Commons, original author: DrBob, redraw: Pbroks13.}
    \label{fig:pinhole_model}
\end{figure}

Due to the projection nature, the measurements in the image are not proportional to the real-world measurements. Far objects are perceived as smaller than near objects. Even circular objects may be perceived as elliptical depending on the viewpoint. Such is the situation with the dartboard, as it is planar but viewed from an angle \cite{Hartley2004}.

\subsubsection{Lens Distortion and Correction}

The real lens does not follow the pinhole camera model. Due to lens distortion, lines in the real world are perceived as curved lines in the camera image. The distortion is most pronounced at the image boundaries. If not corrected for, the dartboard would be mislocated in the image, causing the wrong dartboard section to be registered as hit. Thus, camera calibration is required to correct for lens distortion before any geometric processing is performed \cite{Zhang2000}.

\subsection{Homography and Planar Mapping}

\subsubsection{Coordinate Transformations}

The dartboard is planar, so the projective transformation is used to map the image coordinates to the dartboard coordinates \cite{Hartley2004}.

\subsubsection{Image-to-World Mapping}

During the process of calibration, reference points on the board are defined. Using this information, a mapping is created between the camera pixels and the dartboard positions. After this process is complete, the position of the dart tip can be converted to a position on the board regardless of the angle or distance of the camera. This is a result of the independence of the detection process from the camera angle or distance \cite{Hartley2004}.

\section{Dartboard Geometry}

\subsection{Ring and Sector Structure}

\subsubsection{Standard Dartboard Dimensions}

A dartboard has a series of scoring rings and sector divisions. The dimensions of the dartboard have a constant ratio with regards to each other. Practice boards can have a constant scale for all dimensions. This means that the detection process is independent of the size of the dartboard.

\subsubsection{Sector Indexing and Score Mapping}

Using the information gained during the process of calibration, the dart tip position is assigned a score based on the sector divisions. This is possible due to the independence of the detection process.

\subsection{Coordinate Systems}

\subsubsection{Pixel Space}

The camera provides a position in pixel space. This is a function of the camera's perspective. This information is not suitable for scoring.

\subsubsection{Normalized Board Space}

Using the information obtained during the process of calibration, the dart tip position is expressed in a normalized board coordinate system. This is possible due to the independence of the detection process.

\section{Principles of Motion Detection and Object Tracking}

\subsection{Frame Differencing}

\subsubsection{Temporal Stability}

During the process of motion detection, the camera must be static. Any movement would be expressed as motion in the entire image. This would be misinterpreted by the detection process as a dart strike. This means that the dartboard must be static after the process of calibration.

\subsubsection{Noise Sensitivity}

Lighting conditions play an important role in the detection of motions. The reflections and shadows may be mistaken for movements. Therefore, stable lighting and background are necessary for detecting motions.

\subsection{Blob Detection and Tracking}

\subsubsection{Contour Extraction}

By performing frame subtraction, the moving areas are detectable. The contours of the moving area corresponding to the dart are extracted and analyzed to find the tip of the dart.

\subsubsection{Filtering Strategies}

Filters are used to remove noise from the image. The filters may be tested to find the best filtering strategy to overcome the lighting and noise problems.
